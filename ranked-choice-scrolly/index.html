Consider this example with 100 votes. In ranked-choice voting, a candidate needs at least 50% of votes to win. So if any candidate receives at least 50% of first-choice votes, they automatically win the race.

But in the likely scenario that none of the candidates receive at least 50% of first-choice votes, low-ranking candidates are eliminated and their votes are redistributed.

Once all votes are cast, they are distributed between the nine candidates.

At first, your vote goes to your first-choice candidate.

If your first-choice candidate has enough votes, they will move on to the next round.

But let's say your first choice is eliminated. Then your vote will go to your second-choice candidate.

As candidates are eliminated, their votes are redistributed to voters' next-highest-ranked choice.

Your vote could stay with your second choice candidate, but if they are also eliminated, your vote will go to your third choice, and so on.

Let's say your third-choice candidate never gets eliminated, so your vote ultimately counts toward them.

That doesn't guarantee that they will win the race. A candidate may make it to the final round, but if they are not the first to reach 50% of votes, they still lose the race.

Of course, there is always the scenario where your candidate does win the race.

And a scenario where every candidate you ranked is eliminated, meaning your vote ultimately does not count toward the election.
